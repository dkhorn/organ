## — How a Pipe Organ Works (Quick Explanation)

**Visuals**

- keyboard being pressed
- valve / windchest diagram or footage
- air entering pipe
- pipes of different shapes
- stop knob being pulled

**Voiceover**

The basic idea is simple.

Each pipe plays one note when air flows through it.

Press a key… a valve opens… and air goes into the pipe.  Press a different key, air goes into a different pipe.

Long pipes make low notes. Short pipes make high notes.

Pipes with similar tone are grouped together into a *rank*. A keyboard needs about 61 pipes for a single rank.

The organist turns ranks on and off using *stops*, often combining many ranks at once.

Large organs may have dozens of ranks speaking together — which is what creates that huge symphonic sound.

## 2:25 — A Better Plan

So instead of starting from nothing, I started looking for an instrument I could adopt as a starting point.

Here's where things got interesting. The "used organ" market is a strange place. Large organs are often very integrated with the building they're installed into, and it can be quite costly to extract them and rebuild them somewhere else.

As a result, while a good small studio or chapel organ might run tens of thousands of dollars, you can sometimes find much larger organs listed as basically *free to a good home*.

So why on earth should I start from scratch?

## 3:40 — The Work Begins - Termites

**Visuals**

- termite‑damaged boards
- cleaning parts
- inspecting wood
- photos of the big oven

Before everything was even off the truck, I hit the first surprise: termites.

Some console parts had been stored in rough conditions and picked up some very unwelcome guests. A few pieces were beyond saving and never made it into the shop — but I wanted to rescue as much as possible.

So I built a very large, very improvised oven — about eight feet long — using a space heater, fans, temperature probes, and a thermostat.

Every piece of wood went through it: slowly up to about 135°F internally, then held there for 12 hours.

After that, I didn’t see a single live termite. To be safe, everything also got treated with a borax-based wood insecticide.

The bugs were gone… but the damage remained. Most parts are salvageable with restoration; some will become templates for new ones.

## 4:10 — Pipe Sorting

**Visuals**

- measuring pipes
- laying pipes out
- organizing ranks

At the same time, I needed to figure out what I actually had.

Pipe organs are organized into rows called **ranks** — each one a full set of pipes covering the keyboard.

Each rank has dozens of pipes, and every pipe has a specific place.

So this meant sorting through hundreds of pipes from several different instruments… and putting them back into order.

In parallel, I started designing the organ’s new “central nervous system.”

The original organs used decades-old electrical systems.

Rather than restore all of that directly, the plan is to replace it with a modern control system — new wiring, new drivers, and eventually custom circuit boards.

So while the wood and pipes were being sorted… the electronics were being reinvented.

## 5:20 — The Tonal Plan

With the pipes identified, woodwork stabilized, and a path for modern electronics, I could finally step back and ask: what instrument am I actually building?

The collection had good foundations — about seven ranks — but it leaned heavily toward quieter, more expressive stops.

To get closer to a convincing organ, I needed a stronger principal chorus, real open 8′ tone, and some reeds.

## 7:50 — Escalation

So I went down to take a look.

It’s a large organ, originally built in the 1970s and expanded in the 80's by French organ buliders Casavant — three manuals, pedal, about 23 ranks, and something like 1,800 pipes.

It had suffered from hurricane damage. The console was completely lost, and there’s some water intrusion in parts of the windchests.

But overall… it’s in remarkably good condition — honestly better than some of the Wicks components I’ve already been restoring. And the pipework is excellent.

So… why not.

I committed to rescuing the entire instrument over the next several months.

And instead of adding a few ranks to my earlier design, I'm pivoting to make the Casavant the core of the instrument, with my previous small organ thoughtfully integrated.

So at the end of this, if all goes well, instead of a completely improvised small organ with a fully-jumbled provenance, I will have "Casevant, Opus 3091, 1971; Expanded 1983; Expanded and relocated 2026" [text on screen]
